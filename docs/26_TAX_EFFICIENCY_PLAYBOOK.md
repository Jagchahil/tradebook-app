# 26: Tax Efficiency Playbook, paying yourself and what you can claim

> Written for you as a director and shareholder of a UK limited company, 2026/27 figures. This is how a good accountant would set you up to take money out with the least tax and claim every cost you are genuinely entitled to. I am not your filing accountant. Use this to understand the levers and to brief the person who signs off your accounts. Get them to confirm the numbers for your exact situation before you act.

---

## The one rule that governs everything

A cost is only allowable if it is **wholly and exclusively** for the business. Genuine business costs reduce your corporation tax at 19%, so every pound you correctly claim saves you 19p. Claim all of them, keep the receipt or invoice, and put it through the company.

What you cannot do is run personal spending through the company and call it a business expense. That is not tax efficiency, it is fraud, and HMRC checks. So this playbook is aggressive on the legitimate levers, which are large, and clean on the line. You do not need to cheat to keep your tax low. The legal toolkit already does it.

---

## Part 1. How to take money out, in the right order

Profit comes out of a company in four ways, cheapest first. Use them in this order.

### 1. A director's salary, set at the sweet spot
A salary is a company expense, so it cuts corporation tax. But salary attracts income tax and National Insurance once it gets high, so you keep it low and take the rest as dividends.

- **If you are the only director and have no other employees:** the company cannot claim the Employment Allowance, so the sweet spot salary is about **£6,708** for the year. Above the £5,000 secondary threshold the company pays 15% employer NI, so you stop just high enough to still earn a qualifying year towards your state pension without triggering much NI.
- **If there are two of you as directors, or you have at least one other employee:** the company can claim the **Employment Allowance** (£10,500), which wipes out the employer NI. Now the sweet spot rises to a **£12,570** salary each, using your full personal allowance tax free. This is one quiet reason a two founder company is handier than a one founder company.

Either way the salary is small. The bulk of your pay comes next.

### 2. Dividends, up to the top of the basic rate
After a small salary, take profit as dividends. Dividends are paid from company profit after corporation tax, and they are taxed on you personally but at lower rates than salary, with no NI.

- First **£500** of dividends is tax free (the dividend allowance).
- Up to total income of about **£50,270** the dividend rate is **10.75%**.
- Above that, into the higher band, it jumps to **35.75%**.

So the natural ceiling for a tax light year is to keep your salary plus dividends around **£50,270**. Taking dividends above that is fine, you just pay 35.75% on the slice above, which is still lighter than higher rate salary. Many founders draw to the basic rate top, then leave the rest in the company or pension.

### 3. An employer pension contribution, the biggest lever you are probably ignoring
This is the one that moves the needle. The company pays into a pension for you directly.

- It is a company expense, so it **cuts corporation tax**.
- It carries **no employer NI and no income tax** on the way in.
- It is **not limited by your small salary**, because it is an employer contribution.
- You can put in up to the **£60,000** annual allowance (and possibly carry forward unused allowance from the previous three years), subject to the rules.

So instead of paying yourself a higher dividend you do not need this year and being taxed on it, the company puts money into your pension untaxed. The catch is that it is locked away until pension age. But for tax efficiency on money you do not need to spend now, nothing else comes close. Set this up the moment the company is profitable.

### 4. The director's loan account, for timing
Everything you have personally paid for on the company's behalf, and any money the company owes you, sits in your director's loan account. You can draw that back out tax free, because it is your own money being repaid. Keep a clean record of it.

Be careful the other way. If you take **more** out than the company owes you and the account goes overdrawn, you can trigger a tax charge (known as section 455) and a benefit in kind on the cheap loan. So do not treat the company account as your current account. Pay yourself through salary, dividends, and pension, not by dipping in.

## Part 2. Everything you can legitimately put through the company

Each of these, where it is genuinely for the business, is an allowable cost that reduces corporation tax. This is where "expense a lot" lives, done properly.

**Working from home.** You can either claim the simple HMRC flat rate for working from home, or, if a room is used substantially for the business, a fair proportion of your household bills. The flat rate is the no questions route. The proportional method gives more but needs a sensible calculation.

**Mobile phone and broadband.** Put the phone contract **in the company's name** and the whole cost is allowable with no benefit in kind, one phone per director. Broadband used for the business is claimable too.

**Equipment, computers, tools.** Laptops, monitors, phones, cameras, tools, office furniture. These usually qualify for the **Annual Investment Allowance**, which gives 100% tax relief in the year you buy them. Buy what the business needs and the full cost comes off profit.

**Software and subscriptions.** Your whole stack. Hosting and Vercel, Supabase, the Anthropic and OpenAI API spend, design tools, accounting software, domain and email, anything the business runs on. All allowable.

**Travel and mileage.** Business travel, not your normal commute. If you use your own car for business journeys, claim the HMRC mileage rate, which **rose to 55p a mile for the first 10,000 business miles from April 2026**, then 25p after that. Train, parking, and business trip accommodation are allowable too.

**An electric car through the company.** A genuinely strong perk. The benefit in kind on a pure electric company car is only **4% for 2026/27**, far below a petrol car, and a **brand new electric car gets a 100% first year allowance**, so the company can write off the whole cost against profit in year one. The company can also pay for the charging. If you want a car through the business, make it electric. The numbers are built for it.

**Training and development.** Courses, books, and learning that maintain or improve skills used in the business.

**Professional subscriptions and memberships** relevant to the business, and trade or professional bodies.

**Accountancy and professional fees.** Your accountant, and legitimate legal and professional costs of running the company.

**Pension,** as above. The biggest one, listed in Part 1.

**Trivial benefits.** The company can give you small gifts of up to **£50** each, tax free, as long as they are not a reward for work and not cash. There is a £300 a year cap for directors across the year. Small, but free.

**An annual company event.** The company can spend up to **£150 a head** per year on an annual function, such as a Christmas meal, for staff and directors, tax free.

**Marketing and the cost of getting customers.** Advertising, the Meta and TikTok ad spend when you get there, content tools, and similar promotion costs.

**Bank charges, insurance** (such as professional indemnity and public liability), and the everyday running costs of the company.

**Research and development relief.** Separate and worth real money. The software you are building, with novel AI work, may qualify for R&D tax relief, which gives extra relief on qualifying development costs. Flag this to your accountant specifically. It is easy to leave on the table.

## Part 3. What is not allowable, so you stay safe

Knowing the limits is what keeps the rest clean.

- Everyday clothing, even if you only wear it for work. Only genuine protective gear or branded uniform counts.
- Your normal commute to a regular workplace.
- Client entertaining is not corporation tax deductible (staff entertaining within the £150 rule is).
- Anything with a real personal use element, unless it is correctly treated as a benefit in kind.
- Fines and penalties.

If a cost is part business and part personal, you claim only the business proportion, honestly.

---

## Part 4. The rhythm that keeps it efficient

- Pay a small salary monthly, run it through proper payroll.
- Take dividends in line with available profit, with a dividend voucher and a board minute each time. Paperwork matters if HMRC ever looks.
- Make the employer pension contribution before the company year end to get the corporation tax relief in that year.
- Keep the director's loan account tidy and never let it go overdrawn by accident.
- Use an accountant to file. The fee is itself an allowable expense, and a good one saves more than they cost.
