## The full vision: seven pieces, one roof

### 1. Incorporation
Forming the company itself. **Not FCA or SRA regulated in the UK.** Company formation agents are an unregulated activity, anyone can submit a Companies House incorporation on someone's behalf. This is genuinely one of the cheapest, fastest pieces on this list: an agentic Q&A (sole trader or limited company, share structure, SIC codes, registered office) that submits straight to Companies House. Natural first bundle partner for Lekhio Accountancy, since it is the very first thing a new business needs, and it hands Lekhio Accountancy its first customer the moment the company exists.

### 2. Accountancy
The existing product. Live, proven, and the thing that funds everything else. Nothing here changes.

### 3. Domains and email
Bundled at incorporation. The moment a company is formed, register its domain and stand up business email through a registrar and Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 reseller relationship, so "start a business on Lekhio" ends with an incorporated company, a domain, and a working inbox in one flow, not five separate signups. Commercial reseller deals, no licence needed. Low differentiation on its own, but real value as part of the "one roof" promise, because it removes friction most new owners do not expect to hit.

### 4. Funding
Helping a business raise money: matching to investors, running a raise, arranging a loan. **This is genuinely FCA-regulated territory the moment real matching happens.** "Arranging deals in investments" (article 25 of the Regulated Activities Order) is the exact permission Crowdcube and Seedrs hold to run an investment based crowdfunding platform, and it typically also needs a client money permission. This is not a light-touch vertical. Realistic path: start by referring users to existing authorised lenders and platforms, or a formal partnership with one, rather than building an unauthorised matching or raise-running product. Owning this end to end later is its own FCA authorisation project, the same order of undertaking as the bank.

### 5. Banking, Lekhio Bank
Covered in full below, unchanged from the first pass of this doc: partner on an existing licence first, consider your own EMI or bank licence only once there is real scale and capital.

### 6. Law, Lekhio Law
Covered in full below, unchanged from the first pass: stay informational and drafting only first, no licence needed, consider an ABS or regulated firm partnership only once there is demand and funding for it.

### 7. Exit, helping a business sell
Jag's own definition of "selling" in this list: helping an owner value, prepare, and sell their business when they are ready. This one is genuinely lighter than funding or banking. The Regulated Activities Order carries a specific carve-out, commonly called the **M&A exemption** (article 70), that excludes advising on and arranging the sale of a company from needing FCA authorisation, provided the deal is a real transfer of control, 50% or more of the voting shares, or day to day control, between genuine parties, rather than a portfolio-style investment. Most UK business brokers and small corporate finance firms operate on exactly this exemption without FCA permission. That makes a **Lekhio Exit** product a plausible, lighter-licence vertical: valuation, a teaser document, a buyer-introduction flow, and deal-document assembly feeding off the same drafting engine as Lekhio Law, with a solicitor doing the actual completion paperwork. Still get a lawyer to confirm any specific structure stays inside the exemption before launch, since it stops applying the moment a deal looks like a portfolio investment rather than a trading company sale.

## Going international, an honest read on "by end of year one"

This was in the first pass of this doc and still holds, now applied to the group rather than just the tax product.

**What "global by end of year one" actually means in practice.** Lekhio Accountancy's tax engine is built on HMRC MTD rules, UK CIS, UK dividend and NI thresholds. That is not a translation job, it is a second tax engine, per country, built and exam-tested the way doc 84's Rakha signals were (currently 3,518 tests green on the UK engine alone). Genuinely global, meaning the US, Australia, Canada, and the EU inside 12 months, is not realistic alongside also building out Incorporation, Exit, Funding, Banking, and Law. That is the straight answer.

**What is realistic inside year one: one close market, not "global."** Pick a single market that shares enough of the UK's shape to reuse most of the build. Ireland is the strongest candidate: same language, similar time zone, a self-employed and sole-trader population, a tax authority (Revenue) with a self-assessment-style regime, and Meta's WhatsApp Cloud API works the same way there. That is a genuine, fundable "we are already international" proof point for investors, achievable in year one if it is a real, resourced workstream.

**The honest sequencing:** the UK proves the model and funds the group. Ireland, or a similarly close market, is the year-one international proof point for Lekhio Accountancy. From there, a staged path rather than one big leap:

- **Year one:** Ireland. One close market, proves the model travels.
- **Year two:** a second close market, Australia or Canada are the strongest fits. Both English-speaking, common law, and both run a self-assessment-style regime for the self-employed (the ATO in Australia, the CRA in Canada) close enough in shape to reuse most of the Rakha signal engine's structure, even though the actual tax rules still need their own build and their own exam-style testing.
- **Year three plus:** the US and the wider EU. This is genuinely global, and it is also where the jump in difficulty is real, not just more of the same. The US alone means state-by-state tax variation on top of federal, a different self-employment tax and filing system entirely, and its own banking and legal licensing regimes if Lekhio Bank or Lekhio Law travel with it. Budget this as its own multi-year programme, not a bigger version of the Ireland move.

Taking the wider group (Incorporation, Exit, Funding, Bank, Law) into any new market is its own decision on top of the tax engine, since each regulated piece needs its own licensing regime, market by market, not just a second tax engine.

## The one rule that has to carry across every vertical

You already wrote this rule for the tax product, in CLAUDE.md, before a line of code touched money or filing:

> Money, tax filing, job applications, sending messages on behalf of the user: ALL require explicit human approval. Build the approval gate before the automation it guards. No exceptions.

This is the single most valuable thing Lekhio owns, more than the WhatsApp interface, more than any one feature. It is the model: **the agent watches, gathers, and prepares. The human approves. Nothing irreversible happens without that approval, and the regulatory path is always the recognised one, never a shortcut.** Rakha already proves this works for tax, and it is the model to carry into every product on the list above, agentic and easy on the surface, always with a human approval step underneath anywhere money, a filing, a legal document, or a deal is involved.

The instinct to think this through before a customer is the right one, and it should become the standing rule for every new vertical: **the approval gate, the licensing path, and the honest scope of what the agent is and is not allowed to do get designed and written down before a single customer touches Lekhio Bank, Lekhio Law, Lekhio Funding, or Lekhio Exit, not retrofitted after launch.** For a WhatsApp receipt photo, getting this wrong costs a support ticket. For a bank account, a legal document, an investment deal, or a business sale, getting it wrong is a regulatory or a client-harm event. That has to be settled on paper, with a lawyer or compliance adviser, before build starts, not discovered during build.

## Lekhio Bank, the detail

**Two different routes exist, and they are not the same size of project.**

**Route A: partner, do not licence.** Launch a branded Lekhio Bank current account or card on top of an existing licensed provider, an e-money institution or banking-as-a-service partner who already holds the regulatory permission and the banking rails. Lekhio brands the experience and Rakha runs the categorisation, nudges, and set-aside logic on top, exactly as the app already plans to do with open banking (doc 22). This is how most consumer fintech brands you know actually started, a branded layer on someone else's licence, before some of them later got their own. It needs a commercial and technical integration, not a new regulator relationship, and it can plausibly happen in a year or two on real but modest capital.

**Route B: become the licensed entity yourself.**
- **Become an e-money institution (EMI).** An Authorised EMI needs £350,000 of initial capital, plus ongoing "own funds" of about 2% of the average outstanding e-money balance, a rolling number that grows as the Lekhio Bank float grows. A Small EMI has no minimum capital requirement but is capped in the volume it can handle, useful as a stepping stone, not the end state. New safeguarding rules landing 7 May 2026 also require customer e-money balances to sit in a segregated trust account or be insured, fully ring-fenced from Lekhio's own funds, regardless of route.
- **Become a full bank.** The Bank of England's mobilisation route lets a newly authorised bank operate for up to 12 months with deposit-taking capped at £50,000 total while it finishes building out, which is what most UK challenger banks used to launch. Minimum capital for mobilisation is £1 million, but the actual figure the PRA sets is based on your risk profile and is routinely well above that minimum in practice. This is a multi-year, professionally-advised, seven-figure-plus programme, not a fundraise line item.

**The honest steer:** Route A first. Your own licence is a year two to three ambition once Lekhio has the revenue and the raise to support it. Do not let Lekhio Bank become the thing that stalls Lekhio Accountancy.

## Lekhio Law, the detail

**The line that matters:** the Solicitors Regulation Authority does not need to write AI-specific rules, because reserved legal activities (conduct of litigation, rights of audience, probate work, and a short specific list of others) are already restricted to authorised persons and firms, AI or not. Everything outside that reserved list, plain-English contract review, flagging risky clauses, assembling standard documents, general legal information, can be offered without becoming a regulated law firm, provided it is clearly positioned as information and drafting help, not advice, exactly the same honesty line the tax product already holds with HMRC.

**Two ways to build it:**
- **Stay unregulated, informational only.** A Lekhio Law agent that drafts and flags for the self-employed and small trades, always with "this is not legal advice, see a solicitor for anything that matters" and a clear boundary around reserved activities. Buildable now, no licence needed, reuses the exact prepare-then-human-decides pattern Rakha already runs.
- **Become or partner with an Alternative Business Structure (ABS).** Actually representing clients or giving real legal advice needs SRA authorisation as a law firm or ABS, with a qualified solicitor as the accountable person. A later move, once there is demand and revenue to fund it.

## The Monzo and Revolut question: could this genuinely be a £500m-plus business

Real pushback, and it deserves real numbers, not a bigger version of the same table. Two corrections to how I had been framing this.

**Correction one: the licence was never actually the ceiling.** Revolut ran its entire business, all the way to a $75bn valuation (November 2025 secondary sale) and a projected $9bn of revenue for 2026, on e-money and payment licences. It did not get a full UK banking licence until 11 March 2026, nine years after founding, by which point it already had 65 to 75m-plus customers across 100 countries. Monzo took the other route, a UK banking licence secured early through the mobilisation process (restricted in 2016, lifted in 2017), and grew on that for eight years to reach 15.2 million customers and £1.7bn of revenue in the year to March 2026 (up 39%), before adding a European licence in December 2025. Both routes reach real scale. Route A versus Route B in this doc was never the thing capping the number, it decides what products you can legally offer, not how big the business can get.

**Correction two: the actual ceiling in every table above is user count, and Lekhio's has been sized to the trades and self-employed niche, not to a mass market.** Here is the arithmetic that matters. Both Monzo and Revolut sit at roughly £95 to £112 of revenue per active customer per year (Monzo: £1.7bn over 15.2m customers is £112. Revolut: $9bn over about 75m customers is roughly £95). To clear £500m a year at that same per-customer economics needs **about 4.5 to 5.3 million active banked customers**. To clear £1bn needs **about 9 to 10.5 million.**

The UK's entire self-employed population, every sole trader and small business owner, every trade, landlord, and freelancer combined, is roughly 5 to 6 million people. Getting to Monzo or Revolut scale purely by banking "everyone self-employed in the UK" means something close to total penetration of a market that does not entirely exist yet as banked-by-Lekhio customers, and it still would not leave room for Ireland or any other market on top. **Monzo and Revolut did not get to that scale by serving the self-employed. They got there as general consumer banks that anyone can join**, self-employed people included, but as one segment among tens of millions of ordinary account holders. That is the real mechanism, not a bigger attach rate on Lekhio Accountancy's subscriber base.

**What this actually means for the plan:** £500m-plus is a real, buildable number, but it does not come from pushing the levers in the section above harder. It comes from a different strategic shape entirely: **Lekhio Bank stops being a cross-sell riding on Lekhio Accountancy's trades customer base and becomes its own mass-market consumer and small-business banking product**, acquiring customers directly the way Monzo and Revolut do, competing for the same everyday current account as them, with Lekhio Accountancy as one product sold into that much bigger user base rather than the other way around. That is a different company to run, funded the way they were funded. Revolut has raised $5.89bn cumulative venture capital since 2015. Monzo has raised $1.81bn since its own founding. Both took the better part of a decade of that scale of capital and competition to get where they are now.

**The honest fork, put plainly:** the group vision in this doc, tax to incorporation to a partner-branded bank to law to exit, all built on and cross-sold from a deep, defensible niche in UK self-employed trades, is a genuinely strong, fundable, multi-million-pound business, and years one to five above are a fair picture of it. A Monzo or Revolut-scale outcome is a different, much bigger bet: a mass consumer neobank that happens to have started with a trades tax product, needs its own acquisition engine and its own hundreds-of-millions-to-billions raise, and competes head to head with two extremely well-funded incumbents who have a nine and eight year head start. Both are legitimate ambitions. They are not the same plan, and it is worth deciding on purpose which one Lekhio is actually going after, rather than assuming the niche plan quietly turns into the mass-market one if the numbers are just pushed hard enough.

## Actually, better comps exist, and they change the answer

Jag pushed back again, and this time the pushback surfaces a real error in the last section, not just ambition. Monzo and Revolut were the wrong comparison. They are mass consumer banks, where the huge subscriber count includes tens of millions of casual, low-revenue personal accounts, which drags the average down to about £100 a head. Lekhio is not trying to be everyone's personal current account. It is a **business** product, sold to a self-employed person or a small company, and the right comps are the ones already operating in exactly that lane.

- **Tide**, UK SME business banking: $412.4m revenue, 1.6m business customers globally (UK, India, Germany, France), a blended £200-ish revenue per customer. In the UK alone it holds about 900,000 SME customers, roughly 15% of the entire UK small business market, built over about eight years.
- **Xero**, small business accounting software, the closest pure comp to Lekhio Accountancy: 4.18 million subscribers globally, revenue of roughly NZ$2.7bn to NZ$2.8bn (about £1.3bn), an ARPU that has been climbing and sits somewhere around £250 to £310 a subscriber a year, for software alone, no banking attached, built over about fifteen years from a standing start in New Zealand to a genuinely global subscriber base.

A **business** paying for bookkeeping, a business account, and its admin is worth several times what a casual personal banking customer is worth. That is the correction. ## Yes, and here is exactly why: lending is a different kind of revenue

Good instinct, and it is checkable against Monzo's own real numbers rather than argued in the abstract. **What it could plausibly add, once it is real.** Lekhio has a genuine structural edge here worth naming: a bank built on top of live bookkeeping data can underwrite small business lending better than a blind lender, real-time cash flow, tax set-aside balances, and invoice history are exactly the signals a lender wants and a generic bank does not have. If Lekhio Bank, once fully licensed, earns even Monzo's own £33 or so of lending revenue per customer per year, applied to a group with hundreds of thousands of banked business customers by year eight to ten, that is tens of millions of pounds on top of everything already modelled, and business lending tickets are typically larger than consumer overdrafts, so the real number could plausibly run higher than the consumer benchmark, not lower.

**The honest caveat, because this is exactly how banks blow up if rushed.** A loan book is not a switch you flip, it has to season, Monzo's own £1.9bn book took years of disciplined underwriting to build safely. It is a real, large lever, and it arrives later than everything else in this doc, once the hardest gate (a full banking licence) is actually cleared, not before.

## The free accounting play, and why the bank partner deal moves to NOW (added 11 July 2026)

Jag's question, and it reframes the sequencing in this doc: **the banks give MTD filing away free, so why can't we give the accounting away free if they bank through us?**

The answer is yes, and it is available far sooner than this doc had assumed. Working it through properly.

**First, understand why Monzo can afford free.** Monzo is not giving accounting away. It is buying a deposit with it. When a tradesperson banks with Monzo, Monzo earns interchange on every card swipe, net interest on the balance, and eventually lending income (lending is already 30% of its £1.7bn). The embedded Sage integration is a loss leader funded by a revenue line that only exists because Monzo holds a licence. Free accounting is not a pricing strategy, it is a customer acquisition cost for a bank. Starling with Ember, Tide, and Mettle with FreeAgent are all running the same play. **The real price floor in this market is £0, not the £7 Xero Simple charges, and any competitive analysis that ignores that is lying to itself.**

**Second, and this is the correction: Lekhio does not need its own banking licence to run the same play.** This doc has consistently treated Lekhio Bank as a year 2 to 3 partner move and a year 6 licence. That understated how early the economics arrive, because it conflated two different revenue lines:

- **Interchange** is available on a partner-issued card from day one. It needs a commercial BaaS deal, not a regulator. Route A.
- **Lending (net interest income)** is the one that genuinely requires a full banking licence, because an EMI must safeguard essentially 100% of customer e-money and cannot lend it. Route B.

The free accounting play only needs the first one. That means it is a commercial negotiation we could start this month, not a multi-year authorisation programme.

**Third, the arithmetic, and it holds up.** The key fact is one this doc had not previously established: **UK business and commercial cards sit outside the Interchange Fee Regulation caps.** Consumer debit is capped at 0.20% and consumer credit at 0.30%, but commercial cards are exempt, and interchange on them runs materially higher, commonly quoted in the 1.0% to 2.0% range. The industry-standard BaaS revenue share leaves the fintech brand with roughly 70% of that.

A self-employed tradesperson is a heavy card spender: materials, fuel, tools, plant hire. Run it conservatively.

| Monthly card spend on the Lekhio card | Interchange at 1.0% | Interchange at 1.5% |
|---|---|---|
| £2,000 | ~£14 a month net to Lekhio | ~£21 a month |
| £3,000 | ~£21 a month | ~£31 a month |
| £5,000 | ~£35 a month | ~£52 a month |

(Net figures assume a 70% revenue share to Lekhio after the BaaS partner's cut.)

**Even at the most conservative corner of that table, interchange beats the entire £12.99 subscription.** At realistic trades spend it is two to four times the subscription. That is precisely why Monzo, Tide and Starling can hand the accounting software over for nothing and still make money. The subscription is not the prize. The card is.

**What this changes, concretely:**

1. **The Lekhio Bank partner deal (Route A) moves from a year 2 to 3 item to the highest-leverage unlock on this entire list, and the conversation starts now.** It has no regulatory gate. It is a commercial negotiation with a BaaS provider or EMI. It is the single thing that turns our worst structural weakness (competing against free) into our strongest position (being the free one, with the deepest agent).
2. **The pricing model becomes: paid if you just want the books, free if you bank with us.** That is Monzo's model, run by us, and it defends the £0 floor without destroying the business.
3. **It does not change the £12.99 plan.** The paid tier still exists, still rises once live HMRC filing ships, and still serves everyone who will not move their bank account. Free is a bundle, not a price cut.

**The four honest risks, because this is not free money:**

- **Card capture rate is the whole thing.** The table above assumes their spend actually runs through the Lekhio card. Trades pay by bank transfer, on merchant credit accounts, and in cash. If only 40% of spend lands on our card, halve every number. This is the metric to model before committing, and it is the number a BaaS partner will want to see too.
- **Switching a business bank account is the hardest behaviour change in fintech.** Free accounting is the carrot precisely because the ask is so big. It is not a small favour we are asking in return.
- **Free changes who signs up.** Every free user who never opens an account still costs real AI and WhatsApp money, forever, and pays nothing. This needs the same servicing-cost discipline doc 30 already applies to the trial. Gate free strictly on the account being open and active, not on signup intent.
- **Interchange is under regulatory review.** Commercial card interchange is uncapped today, but the UK regulator is actively looking at card fees, and post-Brexit CNP rates are already under review. If commercial interchange gets capped, the economics of this play compress. Do not build a business that only works at 1.5%, and never make the free tier irreversible.

**Partner or own licence, decided plainly.** Route A first, and it is not close. Interchange, which is what funds free, needs no licence. Lending, which needs the licence, is a year 6 to 8 reward that has to be underwritten carefully and cannot be rushed. Chasing our own banking licence now, to unlock a revenue line we could not safely operate anyway, would stall the accountancy business that pays for everything. Get the partner card live, prove the card capture rate is real, let interchange fund free accounting and the growth it buys, and let *that* traction earn the licensing-grade raise the full bank actually needs. That is the same logic this whole doc has argued, just with the timeline pulled two years forward now that we understand which revenue line sits behind which gate.

## What Lekhio Bank actually is, and the two kinds of authorisation (added 11 July 2026)

Jag's steer, and it is the thing that separates Lekhio Bank from a neobank clone: **Lekhio is not an advisor. It is an agent. It does the work while you sleep, and it asks before it commits.** "We explain what we want to do, you hit run." Two beliefs sit underneath it. First, the tax set-aside should not be a nudge, the bank should just do it. Second, and this is the real conviction: **it is unfair that private banking, forecasting and investment guidance are reserved for people who are already rich.** The same brain that reads a receipt should read the accounts, forecast the year, and tell a plasterer when it makes sense to buy a rental, and exactly what it would do to his tax.

That is a genuinely good product thesis. It also runs straight at the hardest regulatory line in this entire document, so it has to be mapped precisely rather than aspirationally.

### The correction that matters most: the approval gate is not a regulatory shield

This has to be written down before anyone builds on the wrong assumption. **"We prepare it, you approve it" does not take Lekhio outside FCA regulation, and on investments it puts us further inside it.**

- Under **article 53** of the RAO, *advising on investments* is the act of making the recommendation. It is not the execution. If Rakha says "I am going to move £500 into this fund, hit run", **the recommendation has already been made.** A human thumb on the button does not undo it.
- And when they do press run, Lekhio has then also performed **article 25, arranging deals in investments**, which is a separate regulated activity.

So the agentic prepare-then-approve model on investments needs **two** permissions where a plain advisor needs one. The gate makes it more regulated, not less. This is not a loophole to be exploited, it is a design constraint to be respected.

This is not exotic territory. It is exactly what Nutmeg, Moneyfarm and Wealthify are: a machine that prepares a recommendation and executes it on consent. All of them hold article 53 and article 25 permissions. The category is real, established and achievable. It is simply not free.

**The clean way to hold this in your head: there are two kinds of authorisation and Lekhio needs both.**

- **The user's authorisation.** Our moat. The rule in CLAUDE.md. Nothing irreversible happens without a human thumb.
- **The FCA's authorisation.** Our gate. It decides what we are permitted to prepare in the first place.

Pressing run needs both. The first is a product decision already made. The second is a permission we apply for.

### The map, activity by activity

The good news, and it is genuinely good: **most of the agentic bank Jag is describing needs no investment permission at all, because most of it is moving a person's own money around on their own instruction.**

| What Rakha prepares and the user runs | What it actually needs |
|---|---|
| "Set aside £840 for January. Done, it is in your pot." | **The bank, Route A partner card.** Not advice. It only needs the ability to hold and move e-money. |
| "Your cash runs out in six weeks. Chase these three invoices." | **Nothing.** Arithmetic and a message. Buildable today. |
| "Pay this bill. Hit run." | **Payments, via the Route A partner.** Not advice. |
| "Buying a rental would change your tax like this." | **Nothing.** Tax information, held to the same honesty line as everything else. |
| "Now is the time to buy an investment property." | **Nothing under FSMA.** Land and property are **not specified investments** under the RAO (nor are wine, art, stamps or classic cars). Article 53 does not bite. |
| "Here is the mortgage to buy it with." | **Mortgage permissions**, or refer out. Regulated mortgage contracts are regulated. Most buy to let sits outside, unless it is a consumer buy to let. A lawyer decides this line, not us. |
| "People in your position often put surplus into a pension or an ISA." | **FCA Targeted Support permission.** See below. New, live, and applications are open. |
| "Put your surplus into this specific fund. Hit run." | **Full article 53 plus article 25 permission.** The Nutmeg licence. Real, and years away. |

### The open door nobody has walked through yet: Targeted Support

Jag's "it is unfair that investment banking is reserved for the rich" is not just morally right, **it is the FCA's own stated reason for changing the law.** They call it the advice gap.

The FCA's Advice Guidance Boundary Review has produced a new regime called **Targeted Support**. The final rules were confirmed on 26 February 2026 and took effect on **6 April 2026**. Applications for the permission opened on 2 March 2026 and are open now, with a pre-application support service available.

What it permits an authorised firm to do: **make suggestions to groups of customers who share common characteristics**, about pensions and retail investments, without giving full regulated advice and without a per-individual suitability assessment.

That is, close to word for word, the thing Jag wants Khoji to do.

**And Lekhio is unusually well placed to win it, for a reason no wealth manager can copy.** Targeted Support works by segmenting customers into groups with shared characteristics. Every incumbent applying for this will segment on a questionnaire the customer filled in. **Lekhio would segment on verified, live income and expenditure, because we already keep their books.** Our segmentation data is structurally better than theirs. The regime happens to be built around precisely the asset we already have, and it is four months old, so almost nobody has built for it yet.

**Important caveat that stops this becoming a shortcut.** Targeted Support lets us *suggest*. The moment Rakha *executes* the suggestion, we are arranging under article 25 and we need that permission too. Targeted Support is a bridge to the agentic investment product, not a substitute for it.

### The sequencing, and it does not slow the launch

1. **Now, pre launch.** Build nothing. Three cheap things only: complete the ICO registration so the dormant open banking feed switches on (it is how we measure card capture rate for free, and it is how Rakha gets deeper). Keep the set-aside maths visible in WhatsApp, because every "put £840 aside" message a user reads without a pot to put it in is a free demand signal for the bank. And keep launch copy consistent with the bank story: the promise is "we never move your money without asking", which stays true forever, never "we will never touch your money", which we would have to walk back.
2. **Route A partner card.** The agentic own-money bank. The tax set-aside pot, bill payment, cash forecasting. **No investment permission needed.** This is the whole killer feature and it is a commercial negotiation, not an authorisation. See the free accounting play below.
3. **Targeted Support permission.** Once there is a base and a compliance function. The bridge into guidance, and the first honest step towards "private banking for everyone."
4. **Article 53 plus 25.** The full agentic investment product. Years out. Needs real capital and a real compliance team, and it should never be rushed for the same reason a loan book should never be rushed.

### Why the set-aside pot is not a me-too feature

Tide, Starling and ANNA all have a tax pot, so on the surface this looks like parity. It is not, and the difference is the whole company.

**Theirs sets aside a flat percentage. A rule of thumb. A guess.** Ours knows the actual number, because Rakha has the real books, the real CIS deductions, the real allowances and the tax optimiser behind it. "Put 30% aside" is a nudge. **"Put £840 aside, because that is exactly what you will owe on 31 January, and I have already done it, hit run" is a different product entirely.**

That is the flagship feature of Lekhio Bank. It is not investment advice, it needs no FCA advice permission, and it needs nothing but the Route A partner card.

## What to actually do now, without slowing Lekhio Accountancy

0. **Complete the ICO registration and switch on the dormant bank feed (new, 11 July, and this is the only bank-related item that happens before launch).** Roughly £52, and days not months. It is the cheapest item on this entire list and it does three jobs at once: it turns on open banking (built, doc 22, currently gated), it lets us measure the card capture rate from real user data for free before we speak to a single BaaS provider, and it deepens Rakha, which is the moat we already chose. Nothing else bank-related happens before launch.

0b. **Open the Lekhio Bank partner conversation once there are real subscriber numbers (new, 11 July).** Approach BaaS and EMI providers for a branded Lekhio business account and card. It has no regulatory gate, it is a commercial negotiation, and it is what unlocks both the set-aside pot and free accounting funded by interchange. Be honest about the timing though: a UK card programme is typically **3 to 6 months from signing**, and **BaaS providers do not sign pre-revenue companies with no customers.** They will ask what share of a tradesperson's spend lands on our card, and we cannot answer that today. The conversation goes far better at 2,000 paying subscribers with real spend data than it does two weeks before launch with a slide deck. Realistic shape: model the capture rate from the bank feed in months 1 to 3, open conversations months 3 to 6, sign months 6 to 9, card live months 9 to 15.

1. **Register the names**, once a real shortlist exists: Lekhio Accountancy (or whatever it lands on), Lekhio Bank, Lekhio Law, Lekhio Incorporation, Lekhio Exit, plus relevant domains, while cheap and uncontested. Do not rename anything live yet.
2. **Keep Rakha's architecture generic on purpose.** The signal engine, the WhatsApp intent router, and the approval-gate pattern (doc 84) should stay written as reusable scaffolding, not hard-coded to tax only, so every future vertical can sit on top without a rebuild.
3. **Decide the Lekhio-internal holding structure now, on paper, cheap while it is worth little.** Lekhio Ltd as the parent, Lekhio Accountancy Ltd as the first subsidiary, ready to add Lekhio Bank Ltd, Lekhio Law Ltd, Lekhio Incorporation Ltd, Lekhio Exit Ltd as each becomes real. Each regulated vertical needs its own ring-fenced entity, not a shared one, so a problem in one never reaches the others. Confirm this with the accountant alongside whatever doc 28 lands on for the layer above Lekhio, since it is a related but separate conversation.
4. **Write the boundary for Lekhio Law and Lekhio Funding before any code**, with a lawyer's sign-off on scope: what the agent is allowed to draft, flag, or refer, what counts as a reserved or regulated activity it must never touch, and the exact wording of the "not advice" disclaimer. This is the "think it through before a customer" instinct, made concrete, and it applies to Funding as much as Law, since both sit right next to a real regulatory line.
5. **Scope Incorporation and Domains/Email lightly** before building: which formation and registrar APIs to partner with, and the reseller terms for business email, since this is the cheapest, fastest win on the whole list.
